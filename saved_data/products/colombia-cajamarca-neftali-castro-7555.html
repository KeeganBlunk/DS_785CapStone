Colombia Cajamarca Neftali Castro

The cup sweetness is rooted in caramel and honey flavors at City roast level, shifting to sweetened dark chocolate bar at Full City. Top notes range from red honey, Turkish delights, and yellow apple. City to Full City+. Good for epresso.

Overview
Full Cupping Notes

The dry fragrance had a caramel and chocolate sweetness, with slight accents of dried apple and walnut. The wet aromatic have a scent of honey and pastry dough, like baklava, and a tad floral. Running my spoon through the crust let off a subtle accent note of golden raisin too. The cup sweetness is rooted in caramel and honey flavors at City roast level, shifting to sweetened dark chocolate bar at Full City. Top notes emerge as the coffee cools, like red honey, and a honey-fruit confectionary note that reminded me of Turkish Delight. The flavor profile is accented by a yellow apple note that adds a nice juicy quality to fruited acidity, which I also thought was a lot like apple juice. Full City roasts make an amazing espresso. I tend to pull mine pretty short, and the two that I tried were both 18 grams of coffee that yielded 22 and 25 grams over 25 seconds. The chocolate flavors were syrupy and bittersweet, with the creamy richness of chocolate truffles, and subtle fruit notes of lemon and pear.

Farm Notes

Farmer Neftali Castro lives in Cajamarca town, not far from Tolima's capitol city of Ibagu. Cajamarca is known for agricultural, and Neftali would like to keep it that way. He and his neighbors have worked hard to keep mining operations out of their home town, the prospect of gold looming large over those protections. So far they've managed to keep the shovels at bay, in favor of what they see as a much more sustainable future. His farm is called El Diamante, and sits at 1720 meters above sea level, planted almost entirely in Caturra. He employs a fairly typical wet process method of mixing two days of harvest in the fermentation tank, changing the water when adding the coffee from 2nd day which helps to control the fermentation process. The coffee is then moved to raised drying beds, where it dries for 7-10 days. We haven't visited Cajamarca yet, but hope to later this year.
